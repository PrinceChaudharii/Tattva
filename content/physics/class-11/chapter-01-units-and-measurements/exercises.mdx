## Exercises — Chapter 1: Units and Measurements

---

### Exercise 1.1 — Foundational

**Q1.** Fill in the blanks:

(a) The volume of a cube of side 1 cm is equal to \_\_\_\_\_ m³.

(b) The surface area of a solid cylinder of radius 2.0 cm and height 10.0 cm is equal to \_\_\_\_\_ mm².

(c) A vehicle moving with a speed of 18 km h⁻¹ covers \_\_\_\_\_ metres in one second.

(d) The relative density of lead is 11.3. Its density is \_\_\_\_\_ g cm⁻³ or \_\_\_\_\_ kg m⁻³.

**Solution:**

(a) Side = 1 cm = $10^{-2}$ m. Volume = $(10^{-2})^3 = 10^{-6}$ m³.

(b) Radius $r = 2.0$ cm $= 20$ mm, height $h = 10.0$ cm $= 100$ mm.

Surface area = $2\pi r(r + h) = 2 \times 3.14 \times 20 \times (20 + 100) = 1.507 \times 10^4$ mm².

(c) $18$ km h⁻¹ $= \frac{18 \times 1000}{3600} = 5$ m s⁻¹. So 5 metres per second.

(d) Density = $11.3$ g cm⁻³ $= 11.3 \times 10^3$ kg m⁻³ $= 1.13 \times 10^4$ kg m⁻³.

---

**Q2.** Convert the following using dimensional analysis:

(a) A speed of 72 km h⁻¹ into m s⁻¹.

(b) A force of 5 newtons into dynes (1 N = 10⁵ dynes).

(c) An energy of 1 joule into ergs (1 J = 10⁷ ergs).

**Solution:**

(a) $72$ km h⁻¹ $= \frac{72 \times 10^3 \text{ m}}{3.6 \times 10^3 \text{ s}} = 20$ m s⁻¹.

(b) $5$ N $= 5 \times 10^5$ dynes.

(c) $1$ J $= 10^7$ ergs.

### Exercise 1.2 — Foundational

**Q3.** A new unit of length is chosen such that the speed of light in vacuum is unity. What is the distance between the Sun and the Earth in terms of the new unit if light takes 8 min 20 s to reach the Earth?

**Solution:**

Since the speed of light = 1 (new unit per second), and light takes $t = 8 \times 60 + 20 = 500$ s:

$$\text{Distance} = c \times t = 1 \times 500 = 500 \text{ new units}$$

**Q4.** Which of the following is the most precise device for measuring length?

(a) A vernier callipers with 20 divisions on the sliding scale.

(b) A screw gauge of pitch 1 mm and 100 divisions on the circular scale.

(c) An optical instrument that can measure length to within a wavelength of light.

**Solution:**

(a) Least count = $\frac{1 \text{ mm}}{20} = 0.05$ mm $= 5 \times 10^{-5}$ m.

(b) Least count = $\frac{1 \text{ mm}}{100} = 0.01$ mm $= 10^{-5}$ m.

(c) Measures to $\sim 10^{-7}$ m (wavelength of visible light).

The optical instrument is the most precise. ✓

### Exercise 1.3 — Intermediate

**Q5.** A student measures the thickness of a human hair by looking at it through a microscope of magnification 100. He makes 20 observations and finds that the average width of the hair in the field of view of the microscope is 3.5 mm. What is the estimate on the thickness of hair?

**Solution:**

Magnification $M = 100$, observed width $w_{\text{obs}} = 3.5$ mm.

$$\text{Actual thickness} = \frac{w_{\text{obs}}}{M} = \frac{3.5}{100} = 0.035 \text{ mm} = 3.5 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}$$

---

**Q6.** The length, breadth, and thickness of a rectangular sheet of metal are 4.234 m, 1.005 m, and 2.01 cm respectively. Give the area and volume of the sheet to the correct number of significant figures.

**Solution:**

Thickness = $2.01$ cm $= 0.0201$ m (3 significant figures — limiting precision).

Area = $2(l \times b + b \times t + t \times l)$

$= 2(4.234 \times 1.005 + 1.005 \times 0.0201 + 0.0201 \times 4.234)$

$= 2(4.255 + 0.0202 + 0.0851)$

$= 2(4.360) = 8.720$ m²

Rounded to 3 significant figures: **8.72 m²**.

Volume $= l \times b \times t = 4.234 \times 1.005 \times 0.0201 = 0.0855$ m³

Rounded to 3 significant figures: **0.0855 m³** (all three inputs have at least 3 significant figures; thickness has exactly 3, so result has 3).

**Q7.** The mass of a box measured by a grocer's balance is 2.3 kg. Two gold pieces of masses 20.15 g and 20.17 g are added to the box. What is (a) the total mass of the box, and (b) the difference in the masses of the pieces to correct significant figures?

**Solution:**

(a) Total mass = $2.3$ kg $+ 0.02015$ kg $+ 0.02017$ kg $= 2.34032$ kg.

Since the least precise measurement is 2.3 kg (1 decimal place), the total mass = **2.3 kg**.

(b) Difference = $20.17 - 20.15 = 0.02$ g.

Both masses have 2 decimal places, so the difference = **0.02 g**.

### Exercise 1.4 — Intermediate

**Q8.** The parallax of a heavenly body measured from two points diametrically opposite on the Earth's equator is 1.0 minute. If the radius of the Earth is $6.4 \times 10^6$ m, determine the distance of the heavenly body from the Earth.

**Solution:**

Baseline $b = 2R_E = 2 \times 6.4 \times 10^6 = 1.28 \times 10^7$ m.

Parallax angle $\theta = 1.0' = \frac{1}{60}$ degree $= \frac{1}{60} \times \frac{\pi}{180}$ rad $= 2.909 \times 10^{-4}$ rad.

$$D = \frac{b}{\theta} = \frac{1.28 \times 10^7}{2.909 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 4.40 \times 10^{10} \text{ m}$$

---

**Q9.** The period of oscillation of a simple pendulum is $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$. Measured values of $L$ and $T$ are $(20.0 \pm 0.1)$ cm and $(0.900 \pm 0.003)$ s respectively. Calculate the value of $g$ with its uncertainty.

**Solution:**

$g = \frac{4\pi^2 L}{T^2}$

$g = \frac{4 \times 9.8696 \times 0.200}{0.810} = \frac{7.896}{0.810} = 9.75$ m s⁻²

Relative error in $g$:

$$\frac{\Delta g}{g} = \frac{\Delta L}{L} + 2\frac{\Delta T}{T} = \frac{0.1}{20.0} + 2 \times \frac{0.003}{0.900} = 0.005 + 0.0067 = 0.0117$$

$$\Delta g = 9.75 \times 0.0117 \approx 0.11$$

$$g = 9.75 \pm 0.11 \text{ m s}^{-2}$$

### Exercise 1.5 — Advanced

**Q10.** A physical quantity $P$ is related to four observables $a$, $b$, $c$, and $d$ as follows:

$$P = \frac{a^3 b^2}{\sqrt{c} \, d}$$

The percentage errors of measurement in $a$, $b$, $c$, and $d$ are 1%, 3%, 4%, and 2% respectively. What is the percentage error in $P$?

**Solution:**

$$\frac{\Delta P}{P} = 3\frac{\Delta a}{a} + 2\frac{\Delta b}{b} + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\Delta c}{c} + \frac{\Delta d}{d}$$

$$= 3(1\%) + 2(3\%) + \frac{1}{2}(4\%) + 2\%$$

$$= 3\% + 6\% + 2\% + 2\% = 13\%$$

The percentage error in $P$ is **13%**.

**Q11.** If velocity $v$, acceleration $a$, and force $F$ are taken as fundamental quantities, find the dimensions of (a) mass, and (b) time in terms of $v$, $a$, and $F$.

**Solution:**

Given: $[v] = [L T^{-1}]$, $[a] = [L T^{-2}]$, $[F] = [M L T^{-2}]$.

(a) Let $[M] = [v]^x [a]^y [F]^z$:

$[M] = [L T^{-1}]^x [L T^{-2}]^y [M L T^{-2}]^z$

Equating exponents:
- $[M]$: $z = 1$
- $[L]$: $x + y + z = 0$
- $[T]$: $-x - 2y - 2z = 0$

From $z = 1$: $x + y = -1$ and $-x - 2y = 2$.

Adding: $-y = 1$, so $y = -1$, and $x = 0$.

$[M] = [F][a]^{-1} = [F/a]$

(b) Let $[T] = [v]^p [a]^q [F]^r$:

$[T] = [L T^{-1}]^p [L T^{-2}]^q [M L T^{-2}]^r$

Equating exponents:
- $[M]$: $r = 0$
- $[L]$: $p + q + r = 0$
- $[T]$: $-p - 2q - 2r = 1$

From $r = 0$: $p + q = 0$ and $-p - 2q = 1$.

Adding: $-q = 1$, so $q = -1$, and $p = 1$.

$[T] = [v][a]^{-1} = [v/a]$

**Q12.** It is claimed that two cesium clocks, if allowed to run for 100 years, may differ by only about 0.02 s. What does this imply for the accuracy of the standard cesium clock in measuring a time interval of 1 s?

**Solution:**

100 years $= 100 \times 365.25 \times 24 \times 3600 = 3.156 \times 10^9$ s.

$$\text{Fractional error} = \frac{0.02}{3.156 \times 10^9} \approx 6 \times 10^{-12}$$

This means the accuracy of a cesium clock in measuring a 1 s interval is of the order of $\mathbf{10^{-12}}$ — roughly 1 part in a trillion.

### Additional Practice

**Q13.** The farthest objects in our universe discovered by modern astronomers are so distant that light emitted by them takes billions of years to reach the Earth. These objects (known as quasars) have many puzzling features which have not yet been satisfactorily explained. What is the distance in km of a quasar from which light takes 3.0 billion years to reach us?

**Solution:**

$t = 3.0 \times 10^9$ years $= 3.0 \times 10^9 \times 3.156 \times 10^7$ s $= 9.468 \times 10^{16}$ s.

$d = c \times t = 3.0 \times 10^5 \text{ km s}^{-1} \times 9.468 \times 10^{16} \text{ s} = 2.8 \times 10^{22}$ km.
