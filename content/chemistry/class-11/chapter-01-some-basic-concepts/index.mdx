## 1.1 Importance of Chemistry

Chemistry is the branch of science that deals with the composition, structure, properties, and interactions of matter. It plays a central role in science and is connected to biology, physics, geology, ecology, and many other disciplines.

The contribution of chemistry to our daily life is enormous:

- **Medicines** — Development of drugs like cisplatin (anti-cancer), azidothymidine (HIV treatment), and paracetamol.
- **Agriculture** — Fertilisers (urea, ammonium nitrate) and pesticides have enabled the Green Revolution.
- **Materials** — Polymers, semiconductors, superconductors, and nanomaterials.
- **Energy** — Fossil fuels, batteries, fuel cells, and solar cells.
- **Environment** — Understanding ozone depletion, greenhouse gases, and water purification.

> **Key Idea:** Chemistry bridges the microscopic world of atoms and molecules with the macroscopic world of materials and phenomena we observe.

## 1.2 Nature of Matter

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. Matter exists in three physical states:

| Property | Solid | Liquid | Gas |
|---|---|---|---|
| Shape | Definite | Indefinite (takes container shape) | Indefinite |
| Volume | Definite | Definite | Indefinite (fills container) |
| Compressibility | Very low | Low | High |
| Diffusion | Negligible | Slow | Fast |
| Intermolecular forces | Strong | Moderate | Weak |

At the **microscopic level**, matter is made up of atoms and molecules. The state of matter depends on the balance between intermolecular forces and the thermal energy of the particles.

## 1.3 Properties of Matter and Their Measurement

### 1.3.1 Physical and Chemical Properties

**Physical properties** can be measured or observed without changing the identity or composition of the substance — colour, odour, melting point, boiling point, density.

**Chemical properties** require a chemical change to be observed — reactivity with acids, flammability, oxidation state.

### 1.3.2 The SI System

The International System of Units (SI) is used in chemistry. The most relevant base units are:

- **Mass** — kilogram (kg). In chemistry, the gram (g) is more convenient.
- **Length** — metre (m). Commonly used: centimetre (cm), nanometre (nm), picometre (pm).
- **Temperature** — kelvin (K). Also used: degree Celsius (°C), where $T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273.15$.
- **Amount of substance** — mole (mol).

### 1.3.3 Density

Density is a derived quantity:

$$\text{Density} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}}$$

The SI unit is kg m⁻³, but g cm⁻³ is commonly used in chemistry. The density of water at 4 °C is exactly 1.000 g cm⁻³.

## 1.4 Uncertainty in Measurement

Every measurement has some uncertainty. This is expressed using **significant figures**.

### 1.4.1 Scientific Notation

Numbers are expressed in the form $N \times 10^n$, where $1 \leq N < 10$.

- Avogadro's number: $6.022 \times 10^{23}$
- Mass of an electron: $9.109 \times 10^{-31}$ kg

### 1.4.2 Significant Figures Rules

1. All non-zero digits are significant.
2. Zeros between non-zero digits are significant: $1005$ has 4 significant figures.
3. Leading zeros are not significant: $0.025$ has 2 significant figures.
4. Trailing zeros after the decimal point are significant: $2.50$ has 3 significant figures.

### 1.4.3 Arithmetic with Significant Figures

- **Addition/Subtraction:** Result has the same number of decimal places as the least precise term.
- **Multiplication/Division:** Result has the same number of significant figures as the term with the fewest significant figures.

## 1.5 Laws of Chemical Combinations

### 1.5.1 Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier, 1789)

> **"Matter can neither be created nor destroyed."**

In a chemical reaction, the total mass of the reactants equals the total mass of the products.

### 1.5.2 Law of Definite Proportions (Proust, 1799)

> **"A given chemical compound always contains its component elements in fixed ratio by mass."**

For example, water always contains hydrogen and oxygen in the mass ratio 1:8, regardless of its source.

### 1.5.3 Law of Multiple Proportions (Dalton, 1803)

> **"When two elements form more than one compound, the ratios of the mass of the second element that combine with a fixed mass of the first element are ratios of small whole numbers."**

For example, carbon forms two oxides:
- CO: mass ratio O:C = $\frac{16}{12} = 1.33$
- CO₂: mass ratio O:C = $\frac{32}{12} = 2.67$

The ratio of oxygen masses = $2.67 : 1.33 = 2 : 1$ (small whole numbers).

### 1.5.4 Gay Lussac's Law of Gaseous Volumes (1808)

> **"When gases combine, they do so in volumes that bear a simple ratio to each other and to the product if gaseous, all measured at the same temperature and pressure."**

This law led Avogadro to propose that equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules.

## 1.6 Dalton's Atomic Theory

John Dalton (1808) proposed:

1. Matter is made up of indivisible **atoms**.
2. All atoms of a given element are **identical** in mass and properties.
3. Atoms of different elements differ in mass and properties.
4. Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form **compounds**.
5. Atoms can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.

> **Limitations:** Atoms are divisible (subatomic particles). Isotopes mean not all atoms of an element have the same mass. Isobars mean atoms of different elements can have the same mass.

## 1.7 Atomic and Molecular Masses

### 1.7.1 Atomic Mass

The **atomic mass** of an element is the relative mass of its atom compared to the mass of a carbon-12 atom taken as 12 units.

$$\text{Atomic mass unit (u)} = \frac{\text{Mass of one }^{12}\text{C atom}}{12} = 1.66054 \times 10^{-24} \text{ g}$$

### 1.7.2 Average Atomic Mass

Most elements occur as mixtures of isotopes. The **average atomic mass** is:

$$\bar{m} = \sum_{i} f_i \times m_i$$

where $f_i$ is the fractional abundance and $m_i$ is the mass of isotope $i$.

**Example:** Chlorine has two isotopes: ${}^{35}\text{Cl}$ (75.77%, mass = 34.97 u) and ${}^{37}\text{Cl}$ (24.23%, mass = 36.97 u).

$$\bar{m} = 0.7577 \times 34.97 + 0.2423 \times 36.97 = 26.50 + 8.95 = 35.45 \text{ u}$$

### 1.7.3 Molecular Mass

The **molecular mass** is the sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule.

**Example:** Molecular mass of H₂SO₄ = $2(1.008) + 32.06 + 4(16.00) = 98.08$ u.

### 1.7.4 Formula Mass

For ionic compounds (which do not form discrete molecules), we use **formula mass** — the sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in the formula unit.

**Example:** Formula mass of NaCl = $22.99 + 35.45 = 58.44$ u.

## 1.8 Mole Concept and Molar Masses

The **mole** is the SI unit for amount of substance. One mole contains exactly $6.022\,140\,76 \times 10^{23}$ elementary entities (Avogadro's number, $N_A$).

$$\text{Number of moles} = \frac{\text{Given number of particles}}{N_A}$$

$$\text{Number of moles} = \frac{\text{Given mass}}{\text{Molar mass}}$$

The **molar mass** of a substance is the mass of 1 mole of that substance, expressed in g mol⁻¹. Its numerical value equals the molecular mass (or formula mass) expressed in unified atomic mass units.

**Example:** Molar mass of water = $18.015$ g mol⁻¹. One mole of water ($18.015$ g) contains $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ molecules.

---

## 1.9 Percentage Composition

The **mass percentage** of an element in a compound is:

$$\text{Mass \% of element} = \frac{\text{Mass of element in 1 mole of compound}}{\text{Molar mass of compound}} \times 100$$

**Example:** Mass percentage of hydrogen in water:

$$\text{Mass \% of H} = \frac{2 \times 1.008}{18.015} \times 100 = 11.19\%$$

$$\text{Mass \% of O} = \frac{16.00}{18.015} \times 100 = 88.81\%$$

## 1.10 Stoichiometry and Stoichiometric Calculations

Stoichiometry deals with the calculation of masses (and sometimes volumes) of reactants and products involved in a chemical reaction.

### 1.10.1 Balanced Chemical Equations

A balanced equation obeys the law of conservation of mass. For the combustion of methane:

$$\text{CH}_4 + 2\text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{CO}_2 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

The stoichiometric coefficients (1, 2, 1, 2) give the molar ratios.

### 1.10.2 Limiting Reagent

In most reactions, one reactant is present in a lesser amount than required by stoichiometry. This is the **limiting reagent** — it determines the amount of product formed.

**Example:** If 2 mol of CH₄ is reacted with 3 mol of O₂:

From the equation, 1 mol CH₄ requires 2 mol O₂. So 2 mol CH₄ requires 4 mol O₂. But only 3 mol O₂ is available. Hence, **O₂ is the limiting reagent**.

Product formed: $\frac{3}{2} = 1.5$ mol of CO₂.

### 1.10.3 Concentration of Solutions

**Molarity** (M) is the most common unit of concentration:

$$\text{Molarity} = \frac{\text{Moles of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution in litres}}$$

Other units include molality (m), mole fraction, and mass percentage.

## 1.11 Summary

- Chemistry is the study of matter — its composition, structure, properties, and transformations.
- The SI system provides standard units for measurement; significant figures communicate precision.
- The laws of chemical combination (conservation of mass, definite proportions, multiple proportions, gaseous volumes) form the experimental basis of atomic theory.
- Dalton's atomic theory explains these laws but has known limitations.
- Atomic and molecular masses are relative, based on the carbon-12 standard.
- The mole concept links the microscopic (atoms) and macroscopic (grams) worlds.
- Stoichiometry allows quantitative prediction of chemical reactions.
- The limiting reagent determines the maximum product that can be formed.
